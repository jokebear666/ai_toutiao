  • [arXiv251229] SENTINEL: A Multi-Modal Early Detection Framework for Emerging Cyber Threats using Telegram

    • tags: [sec], [cyber threat intelligence], [multi-modal fusion, large language models, graph neural networks, early detection, social media analysis]

    • authors: Mohammad Hammas Saeed, Howie Huang

    • institution: George Washington University

    • link: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.21380

    • contributions: 1. Proposes SENTINEL, a multi-modal framework for early cyber threat detection by aligning social media discussions with real-world attacks. 2. Combines language modeling (using LLMs) and network coordination analysis (using GNNs) to fuse textual and relational signals from platforms like Telegram. 3. Demonstrates the framework's effectiveness on a dataset of 365k messages from 16 Telegram channels, achieving an F1 score of 0.89 for threat alignment.

    • thumbnail: https://pub-9ba4a5dae3bf4fc7b7d26411a74e92db.r2.dev/thumbnails/c3e47a235de6afcb4955afd774a9e4b3883efcafc7e4aaa97649575ef2fb34d0_w640_q70.webp

    • Simple LLM Summary: The paper presents SENTINEL, a framework for the early detection of cyber threats by analyzing multi-modal signals from social media platforms like Telegram. It combines large language models for text understanding with graph neural networks to model user coordination, successfully aligning online discussions to real-world attacks. The evaluation on Telegram data shows the approach is effective, achieving a high F1 score of 0.89.

    • tags: [sec], [social network security], [Graph Neural Networks, influence campaigns, temporal link prediction, troll detection, Reddit]

    • authors: Mohammad Hammas Saeed, Isaiah J. King, Howie Huang

    • institution: George Washington University

    • link: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.21391

    • contributions: 1. Proposes ALETHEIA, a system that formalizes the detection of malicious accounts in influence campaigns as a node classification and link prediction problem using a graph-based representation. 2. Demonstrates that a detection pipeline combining topological (graph) and linguistic features outperforms standard interaction and user features, achieving a 3.7% F1-score improvement. 3. Introduces a novel temporal link prediction mechanism for influence campaigns by stacking a GNN over an RNN to forecast future troll interactions (TTE/TUE) with high accuracy (96.6% AUC).

    • thumbnail: https://pub-9ba4a5dae3bf4fc7b7d26411a74e92db.r2.dev/thumbnails/473a865358e998c62938f17660edc395a7658bf360ef15e62ea79317e8734aec_w640_q70.webp

    • Simple LLM Summary: This paper presents ALETHEIA, a system that uses Graph Neural Networks (GNNs) to detect malicious accounts and predict their future interactions in social media influence campaigns. By modeling campaigns as graphs and combining structural and linguistic features, it improves detection performance and forecasts troll behavior with high accuracy. The results underscore the importance of leveraging network structure to combat coordinated malicious activity online.

It proposes a new centrality metric (HCTCD) and uses beta regression to analyze data from NeurIPS, ICML, and ICLR. The findings show that long-term network centrality, especially at the team level, is a strong predictor of citations, challenging evaluation paradigms that focus solely on content.

    • tags: [ai], [evolutionary game theory], [agent-based model, tag-based cooperation, habitat fragmentation, Snowdrift game, Monte Carlo simulation]

    • authors: Hui Zhang, Tarik Hadzibeganovic, Xiao-Pu Han

    • institution: Northwestern Polytechnical University, University of Graz, Hangzhou Normal University

    • link: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.22077

    • contributions: 1. Developed a spatially explicit agent-based model to study the interplay between habitat fragmentation, phenotypic diversity, and the evolution of cooperation. 2. Demonstrated that heterogeneous fragmentation of empty sites, combined with high phenotypic diversity and low cost-to-benefit ratios, suppresses ethnocentric cooperation while promoting unconditional altruism. 3. Showcased the "anti-fragility" of cooperators, revealing they can benefit from harsh environmental conditions in fragmented, phenotypically diverse populations.

    • thumbnail: https://pub-9ba4a5dae3bf4fc7b7d26411a74e92db.r2.dev/thumbnails/6018dfd5e7024b70b1b8b9e06b12d574297ce6358fbc3334144e9bf587f3cea9_w640_q70.webp

    • Simple LLM Summary: This paper investigates how habitat fragmentation and phenotypic diversity influence the evolution of cooperation using a spatially explicit agent-based model based on the Snowdrift game. The simulations show that a combination of high phenotypic diversity, low interaction costs, and moderate fragmentation of empty sites strongly promotes unconditional cooperation while suppressing conditional, ethnocentric strategies. The main conclusion is that heterogeneous habitat fragmentation can act as a potent cooperation-promoting mechanism, demonstrating the resilience of cooperators in degraded environments.

  • [arXiv251230] Information is localized in growing network models

    • tags: [ai], [network science], [growing network models, information localization, neural density estimators, graph neural networks, Bayesian inference]

    • authors: Till Hoffmann, Jukka-Pekka Onnela

    • institution: Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health

    • link: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.23622

    • contributions: 1. Theoretically establishes that information about model parameters is localized in small subgraphs for a broad class of growing network models. 2. Develops neural density estimators (NDEs) using graph neural networks (GNNs) with limited receptive fields to approximate posterior distributions for likelihood-free inference. 3. Empirically validates the localization theory on nine models and shows NDEs can achieve high-fidelity inference even for non-localized models at a fraction of the cost.

    • Simple LLM Summary: This paper addresses the challenge of intractable likelihoods in mechanistic growing network models. It proposes that information about model parameters is localized in small subgraphs and leverages this by developing neural density estimators based on graph neural networks with limited receptive fields for Bayesian inference. The main conclusion is that information localization is a fundamental property of network growth, justifying the use of local subgraph analysis and limited-receptive-field GNNs for efficient and accurate inference.

  • [arXiv251230] R&D Networks under Heterogeneous Firm Productivities

    • tags: [other], [economic theory, industrial organization], [R&D networks, network formation, pairwise stability, positive assortative matching, heterogeneous productivity]

    • authors: M. Sadra Heydari, Zafer Kanik, Santiago Montoya-Blandón

    • institution: University of Glasgow (Adam Smith Business School)

    • link: https://arxiv.org/pdf/2512.23337

    • contributions: 1. Introduces heterogeneous R&D productivities into an endogenous R&D network formation model, generalizing a classic framework. 2. Shows that large productivity gaps destabilize the complete network and make positive assortative (PA) networks stable, overturning benchmark results. 3. Demonstrates a counterintuitive welfare result where higher average productivity can lead to lower welfare due to network structure changes and crowding-out effects.

    • thumbnail: https://pub-9ba4a5dae3bf4fc7b7d26411a74e92db.r2.dev/thumbnails/4de1f94926ff207ccf13bed13e57cff151e461d844157a749e33a8f96c2612df_w640_q70.webp

    • Simple LLM Summary: This paper extends an R&D network formation model by incorporating heterogeneous firm productivities. It finds that large productivity differences lead to the instability of fully connected networks and the stability of networks where firms cluster by productivity (Positive Assortative networks). The key conclusion is that higher average R&D productivity can paradoxically reduce overall welfare by inducing less efficient network structures or crowding out effects.
